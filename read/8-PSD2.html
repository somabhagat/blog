PSD2, SCA, and the AI Agent Revolution: Navigating Europe's Regulatory Bedrock

Strong Customer Authentication was designed for humans. Delegated Authority is being designed for machines. Here is what changes — and what cannot — when an AI agent becomes the one signing the transaction.

In Europe, you do not move money without a deep respect for the regulatory framework. PSD2 — the Second Payment Services Directive — is not merely a compliance checkbox. It is the structural foundation upon which every modern payment innovation in Europe is built. From open banking to instant payments to, now, AI agent execution, everything flows through this bedrock.

The Santander–Mastercard AI agent pilot that completed Europe's first live end-to-end agentic payment did not happen in a regulatory vacuum. It happened because the right frameworks were in place, and because those frameworks are beginning to evolve to accommodate what comes next. Understanding that evolution — and its limits — is essential for anyone building in payments right now.

The regulatory bedrock: what PSD2 actually requires

PSD2 is often described as an "open banking directive." It is more accurate to call it a trust architecture — a legally mandated set of rules that governs who can touch money, under what conditions, and with what accountability.

At its core, PSD2 does three things. It mandates open access to payment account data via standardised APIs — creating the open banking ecosystem. It defines two new categories of licensed payment service provider: Account Information Service Providers (AISPs) and Payment Initiation Service Providers (PISPs). And it mandates Strong Customer Authentication for any electronic payment, making security a legal requirement rather than a product decision.

For payments professionals working across the DACH region, PSD2 compliance is table stakes. But the interesting question is not what PSD2 requires today — it is how its requirements interact with the emerging reality of AI-initiated transactions.

The SCA paradox: designed for humans, applied to machines

SCA was built on a fundamental assumption: a human is present at the point of payment. That assumption is now being stress-tested.

Strong Customer Authentication requires at least two of three factors — something you know, something you have, something you are. Every one of those factors was designed for a human being. A PIN is something a person knows. A phone is something a person possesses. A fingerprint is something a person inherently is.

When an AI agent initiates a payment, none of those factors apply in their traditional form. The agent does not know a PIN in the way a human does. It does not possess a device. It certainly does not have a fingerprint. And yet — as the Santander–Mastercard pilot demonstrated — the transaction can be completed securely. The question is: what replaces the human authentication factors?

Traditional SCA
  • Human present at point of payment
  • PIN or password entered manually
  • Device possession confirmed via OTP
  • Biometric check on phone or terminal
  • Authentication is a moment in time
  • Challenge always visible to cardholder
Delegated Authority (AI)
  • AI agent acts within pre-approved mandate
  • Cryptographic credential replaces PIN
  • Tokenised identity issued by network
  • Risk scoring replaces biometric check
  • Authentication is an ongoing policy
  • Human oversight at mandate level, not transaction

The answer emerging from frameworks like Mastercard Agent Pay is Delegated Authority. Instead of authenticating at the moment of each transaction, the human authenticates once — at the moment they establish the agent's mandate. That mandate defines what the agent can do, up to what limits, under what conditions. The agent then operates within those bounds, and the trust is carried by cryptographic credentials rather than human biometrics.

The regulatory friction: BaFin, FINMA, and the AI signature problem

Here is where theory meets the hard wall of national regulation. PSD2 is a European directive — but it is implemented differently in each member state. In Germany, BaFin (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht) is the enforcer. In Switzerland, FINMA (Finanzmarktaufsichtsbehörde). Each has its own interpretation, its own supervisory culture, and its own tolerance for novel payment mechanisms.

The specific challenge for AI-initiated transactions is what regulators are calling the "AI signature problem": when an AI agent executes a transaction, who is legally responsible? PSD2's liability framework places responsibility on the PSP for unauthorised payments. But what constitutes "unauthorised" when the authorisation was granted to an agent, not a specific transaction?

🏛
BaFin (Germany) — high scrutiny model

BaFin's supervisory approach emphasises auditability and documented liability chains. For AI-initiated payments, this means the entire decision path — why the agent triggered the payment, what mandate authorised it, what credential was used — must be loggable and reconstructable. BaFin is not hostile to innovation, but it requires evidence that controls are operational before scale.

🏔
FINMA (Switzerland) — principles-based approach

Switzerland's FINMA operates on a principles-based regulatory model. Rather than prescribing specific technical implementations, it sets outcomes: consumer protection, system stability, market integrity. This gives more flexibility to innovate — but also places more responsibility on institutions to demonstrate that their AI frameworks achieve those outcomes. The Swiss market's voluntary open banking model reflects this philosophy.

📋
PCI-DSS — the floor beneath both

Regardless of jurisdiction, any system handling cardholder data must comply with PCI-DSS. For AI agent frameworks, this creates specific obligations around how credentials are stored, how tokenisation is implemented, and how the agent's access to payment data is scoped and logged. Compliance here is not optional — it is the trust infrastructure that makes the entire model viable.

The AI signature problem is not a technical challenge — it is a legal one. Regulators need to know who is responsible when an algorithm executes a payment. The answer must be traceable to a human decision, even if that decision was made weeks before the transaction.

The Swiss divergence is the most instructive case study. Without a PSD2 mandate, Switzerland's open banking evolution has been driven by market participants rather than regulation. The result is a different architecture: banks open their interfaces voluntarily, the standards are industry-defined rather than legally prescribed, and innovation happens at a different pace and in different directions.

The marktwirtschaftlicher Ansatz — the market-driven approach — means that Switzerland's payment ecosystem has developed genuinely distinctive products. TWINT, the dominant Swiss mobile payment solution, has no direct equivalent elsewhere in Europe. QR-Rechnung is a Swiss-specific invoicing innovation with wide adoption. SIC5, Switzerland's instant payment infrastructure, operates on its own technical standards. For a payments product leader working across DACH, this diversity is not an inconvenience — it is a design constraint that must be embedded in every product decision from day one.

Compliance as a product feature

There is a tendency in fintech to frame regulatory compliance as friction — something that slows you down, adds cost, and constrains product design. That framing is wrong, and experienced payments professionals know it.

In a market built on trust, compliance is the product. When a corporate treasurer decides which payment platform to use for cross-border payroll, they are not just comparing features and fees. They are assessing which provider has the most credible controls, the most reliable audit trail, and the most robust liability framework. PCI-DSS certification is not a badge — it is a signal that you have earned the right to be trusted with money.

The compliance-as-product checklist
  • PSD2 licence (AISP or PISP) as a customer trust signal, not just a legal requirement
  • SCA implementation that minimises friction while maximising security — the 3DS2 silent authentication path
  • PCI-DSS Level 1 certification published and accessible to enterprise prospects
  • BaFin / FCA / FINMA regulatory correspondence handled proactively, not reactively
  • Audit trail architecture designed from day one — not retrofitted after an incident
  • Consumer protection terms that exceed the PSD2 minimum — a differentiator in enterprise sales

What comes next: PSD3 and the agentic payments framework

PSD2 was not designed for AI agents. Its successor — PSD3, currently in European legislative process — is beginning to grapple with these questions. The key areas where PSD3 and accompanying regulatory technical standards will need to evolve:

1
Delegated Authority standards

PSD3 will need to define what constitutes valid "delegation" of payment authority to an AI agent — what the mandate must contain, how it must be authenticated, and what limits apply. The existing framework for recurring payments (PSD2 RTS Article 10) provides a partial template, but agentic payments require a more comprehensive treatment.

2
Liability allocation for AI-initiated transactions

PSD2's liability framework was designed for human-initiated payments. When an AI agent makes an error — executes a payment outside its mandate, or is compromised — the question of who bears liability needs a clear regulatory answer. PSD3 discussions are beginning to address this, but resolution is likely years away.

3
Open Finance expansion

Beyond payment accounts, Open Finance extends the PSD2 principle to investment accounts, insurance, pensions, and mortgages. For AI agents that manage holistic financial portfolios, this expanded data access is essential — but it brings additional regulatory complexity around data governance, consent management, and cross-sector liability.

4
EU AI Act interaction

AI systems used in payment decisions may fall under the EU AI Act's high-risk category, triggering requirements for transparency, human oversight, and conformity assessment. Payments professionals should assume that AI-driven payment orchestration will face dual regulatory scrutiny from both financial and AI regulators simultaneously.

PSD2 opened the banking rails. PSD3 will need to answer who is responsible when an algorithm drives the train.
