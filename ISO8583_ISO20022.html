ISO 8583, ISO 20022, and Modern Payment APIs: The Language of Money Explained

Every payment that ever completed successfully relied on a messaging standard that nobody but the engineers in the room ever thought about. After 13 years of building payment products, here is how I explain the plumbing — and why the industry is in the middle of its biggest messaging upgrade in 40 years.

Payments Engineering
10 min read

When you tap your card at a terminal, something happens in the background that most people — including most people who work in fintech — never see. A message leaves the terminal, travels through an acquirer, crosses a card network, reaches your issuing bank, gets approved or declined, and returns an answer — all in under two seconds, across potentially four countries and six systems, using a message format that was standardised in 1987.

That message format is ISO 8583. And it is both one of the most impressive pieces of engineering in financial services — and one of the most overdue for replacement.

Understanding the messaging standards that underpin global payments is not just a technical exercise. It is the foundation for understanding why certain products are easy to build and others are not, why cross-border payments are expensive, why fraud rules are imprecise, and why the payments industry is currently in the middle of its most significant infrastructure upgrade since the introduction of chip cards.

The big picture: why messaging standards matter

Before diving into the standards themselves, it helps to understand what problem they are solving — and why having a standard matters at all.

Imagine two banks trying to exchange information about a payment. Bank A needs to tell Bank B: "Customer X has sent €500 to Customer Y, from account 12345, at 14:32 on 18 March 2026, reference INV-9876." Without a shared standard, Bank A and Bank B would need a custom integration. Multiply that by the number of banks, payment processors, card networks, and fintech companies in the world, and you have an exponential problem.

Messaging standards solve this by creating a common language that all participants agree to use. The challenge is that payments has never had just one language. It has had several, each designed for a different era and a different set of problems.

ISO 8583: the language your card speaks

ISO 8583 is often described as the "morse code of payments" — simple, fast, and to the point, but lacking the richness that modern systems demand.

ISO 8583 was created in 1987 to solve a specific problem: how do you make a POS terminal in a supermarket talk to a bank's authorisation system in milliseconds, reliably, at massive scale? The answer was a compact, binary message format using a bitmap structure — a fixed-length control field where each bit indicates whether a particular data element is present.

The elegance was its efficiency. An ISO 8583 authorisation request might be only a few hundred bytes. Visa must process authorisations in milliseconds across a network of 7 billion endpoints — and ISO 8583 was engineered to make that possible. The problem is that every network has customised it differently. Mastercard's implementation is not identical to Visa's, which is not identical to any given bank's internal version. Connecting to a new card network is never just "speaking ISO 8583" — it requires months of integration work.

ISO 20022: the language the banking system is learning

While ISO 8583 solved the card terminal problem, a completely separate problem existed in corporate and interbank payments: they had no common language at all. SWIFT's MT format handled international wires. Each country had its own domestic format — BACS in the UK, ACH in the US, SEPA in Europe. None shared data compatibly. Moving money internationally meant messages being truncated, fields lost in translation, and human intervention required.

ISO 20022 was designed from the ground up to solve this. First published in 2004, it uses XML (and now JSON) with named and tagged fields. Every piece of information is explicitly labelled. A machine reading an ISO 20022 message can understand what every element means without a lookup table — enabling true Straight Through Processing (STP).

REST APIs: the developer interface to the payment world

ISO 8583 and ISO 20022 are the languages that financial institutions speak to each other. REST APIs are the language that developers use to access payment capabilities — and they exist at a completely different layer of the stack.

When a fintech company builds a payment product using Stripe, they never touch ISO 8583. They call POST /v1/charges and Stripe handles everything beneath — the token management, the card network routing, the ISO 8583 messages, the settlement. The REST API is the abstraction layer that made fintech accessible to developers who had never heard of a bitmap.

Comparison: the fundamental differences

ISO 8583 ISO 20022 REST APIs
Indicative capability score per dimension (0–10). Based on technical specifications and industry assessments.
⚡
Speed: ISO 8583 wins

ISO 8583's compact bitmap format was engineered for sub-second authorisation at massive scale. ISO 20022's XML messages are significantly larger — making them unsuitable for high-volume consumer card transactions at POS. REST APIs add variable latency depending on the abstraction layer.

📊
Data richness: ISO 20022 wins

ISO 20022 carries vastly more structured information — full debtor/creditor details, purpose codes, remittance information, regulatory identifiers. The richer the data, the better the fraud detection model. ISO 8583 is constrained by fixed field definitions designed before real-time AML screening existed.

🔧
Developer experience: REST APIs win

No developer picks up ISO 8583 documentation and builds something in an afternoon. REST APIs — particularly Stripe's — were designed with the developer as the primary user. JSON is readable. HTTP is universal. This abstraction is what democratised payment development.

🌍
Interoperability: ISO 20022 wins

Over 70 countries have migrated domestic systems to ISO 20022. SWIFT completed cross-border migration in 2022. FedNow, RTP, SEPA Instant — all ISO 20022 native. ISO 8583 is universal within card payments but has no interoperability with bank transfer systems.

ISO 8583 is morse code: fast, efficient, and to the point — but limited and open to interpretation. ISO 20022 is a 10-page report: rich, structured, and machine-readable — but slower and more expensive to process.

— Adapted from Konstantin Dubovitskiy, AFT Finance

The migration: why the industry is switching — slowly

The case for migrating from ISO 8583 to ISO 20022 is intellectually compelling. The execution is not. Every POS terminal, ATM, acquirer integration, issuer authorisation system, and fraud engine was built around ISO 8583. The integration burden runs into hundreds of billions of dollars of installed infrastructure. The coexistence period is measured in decades, not years.

Key ISO 20022 migration milestones — global
  • SWIFT cross-border payments: ISO 20022 live November 2022, legacy MT formats supported through 2025
  • SEPA Credit Transfer and Direct Debit: ISO 20022 XML since 2008, extended to Instant Credit Transfer (SCT Inst)
  • UK CHAPS (high-value): ISO 20022 migration completed 2023
  • FedNow (US instant payments): ISO 20022 native from launch, July 2023
  • Swiss SIC5 (instant): ISO 20022 native, operational 2024
  • Australia NPP (New Payments Platform): ISO 20022 from inception, 2018
  • Card networks (Visa, Mastercard): ISO 20022 at cross-border and premium tiers; ISO 8583 remains for core card authorisation — full timeline uncertain

What this means for payment product builders

🏗
You will work with all of them simultaneously

No payment product operates in a single standard. A cross-border payout might use ISO 20022 for the SWIFT leg, ISO 8583 for card network authorisation, a Visa REST API for push-to-card disbursement, and a PSD2 Open Banking API for account funding. Understanding where each standard lives in your transaction flow is the prerequisite for designing a reliable product.

🔄
Translation is a hidden cost

When a payment crosses between ISO 8583 and ISO 20022 domains — which happens constantly in cross-border flows — data gets truncated. ISO 8583's limited fields cannot carry all the information in an ISO 20022 message. This data loss is the root cause of many compliance failures, fraud false positives, and reconciliation exceptions.

📈
ISO 20022 data is a competitive advantage

Organisations that can receive and process ISO 20022 messages have access to richer transaction data. That advantage compounds: better fraud models, better AML screening, better reconciliation automation. The migration cost is real; so is the long-term advantage.

The question is not whether your payment product will need to handle ISO 20022. It is whether you design for it from the start — or retrofit it when you have to.
