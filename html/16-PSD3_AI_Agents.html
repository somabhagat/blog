The PSD3 / PSR Paradox: Why AI Agents Are About to Break Strong Customer Authentication

Strong Customer Authentication (SCA)[1] was built on the fundamental assumption that a human is looking at a screen to authorise a transaction. What happens when an autonomous AI Agent makes a real-time purchasing decision? Here is why Europe's next payment frameworks are on a collision course with the machine economy.

Payments Engineering & Strategy
16 min read

Imagine this: It is 2:00 AM. An autonomous AI procurement agent detects a sudden 15%[c] pricing discount on a critical API cloud service. The agent makes a real-time purchasing decision to prepay for bulk credits, dynamically routing the payment through a payment orchestration platform (POP) to optimize transaction costs.

3D Secure (3DS)[2] challenges. It demands a Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) factor: a face scan, a fingerprint, or a one-time passcode (OTP) sent to a mobile phone.

But there is no human at the screen. The AI agent cannot feel a biometric sensor, nor can it open an SMS app. The transaction times out. The bargain is lost. The autonomous economy is frozen.

This is the true PSD3 / PSR Paradox[3]. The upcoming Third Payment Services Directive (PSD3) and Payment Services Regulation (PSR) do not make autonomous payments outright illegal. Instead, they introduce a massive compliance and architectural design hurdle. The regulation is forcing a paradigm shift away from fragile, human-centric triggers toward advanced technical frameworks. It is not a legal ban, but a governance challenge that reshapes how agentic commerce must be built.

  • Trusted Beneficiaries: One-time human verification adds platforms to an explicit whitelist, avoiding future prompts.

Structural Friction in Human-Centric Rules
  • Inherence Factor Dilemma: Background servers executing transactions cannot supply real-time biological biometrics.
  • Provider Liability Shift: Massive liability updates make risk officers naturally block non-human behavioral profiles.
  • 180-Day Consent Wall: Explicit account re-authentication requirements prevent "set-it-and-forget-it" data scanning.

The Liability Matrix: Who Bears the Cost of an Agent Dispute?

When an autonomous AI agent executes a transaction that the end-customer later disputes, the industry enters a complex legal and operational gray area. Looking at this through the lens of payment infrastructure, regulatory compliance, and card scheme rules, this is a fundamental restructuring of the law of agency and payment authorization.

Under current European frameworks (PSD3/PSR, BaFin guidance, and SEPA/Card Scheme rules), a payment is digitally binary: it is either authorized or unauthorized. There is no legal middle ground or category for "my autonomous bot bought it, but it made an unoptimized or flawed purchasing decision."

A2A Schemes (SEPA Instant & Wero)

Card Schemes vs. Account-to-Account (A2A) Allocation

Card Networks: The pivot point for liability under network rules remains the initial validation footprint. When a customer configures their AI agent and links a card, they undergo full SCA (biometrics or 3D Secure). If subsequent transactions are properly flagged by the merchant as Merchant Initiated Transactions (MITs) using the original network transaction ID, the cardholder's issuing bank cannot execute a chargeback under Reason Code 10.4 (Other Fraud). The buyer remains fully liable for the agent’s micro-actions.

A2A Schemes: Under BaFin and European Payments Council rules, instant settlement behaves like digital cash. If an agent executes a transaction via an Open Banking Variable Recurring Payments (VRP)[5] or dynamic SEPA mandate, the transaction is legally considered authorized. Unless the buyer can prove an external network security breach or cyber-theft, a malfunctioning bot algorithm is considered a commercial contract issue, not banking fraud. The bank will not reverse a SEPA Instant settlement.

Advanced Edge Cases: When Controls Fail

Even with strict limits established, systemic technical loops or edge vulnerabilities can cleanly bypass traditional payment safety controls:

Edge Case A: The AI Hallucination & Micro-Transaction Attack
Scenario: A buyer caps agent single transactions at €100 and daily expenditure at €1,000. Following an adversarial hallucination or an unhandled AI retry block, the agent fires off 99 consecutive transactions of €9.99 each within a 3-minute window to the exact same merchant terminal endpoint.

The Catch & Liability: Technically, every isolated transaction complies perfectly with the €100 limit, and the aggregate total (€989.01) sits right below the daily threshold cap. Because the merchant acted in good faith, providing immediate automated compute or digital service access, and standard PSP transaction stacking rules weren't tripped, this is a legally binding debt. The buyer is on the hook.
Edge Case B: The Elastic Scope Creep
Scenario: An autonomous enterprise engine is provisioned to source "AI cloud infrastructure credits up to €500." The endpoint provider dynamically updates its standard master services agreement inline, bundling "Premium Security Monitoring" directly into the credit transaction line items. The agent automatically executes the purchase and signs the modified click-through agreement to resolve the target goal.

The Catch & Liability: The spending ceiling wasn't broken, but the agent contractually bound the principal enterprise to an expanded, unvetted corporate liability framework. If the programmatic authority token allowed the agent to bypass standard document legal reviews to complete execution, the customer remains legally bound.

Cryptographic Protection Strategies

To confidently navigate these structural risks, both corporate buyers and processing merchants must shift away from lagging, retrospective dispute monitoring toward proactive, real-time cryptographic boundaries.

  • For Enterprise Buyers: Deploy Variable Recurring Payments (VRP) featuring hyper-restrictive parameters—limiting not just raw fiat amounts, but checking explicit IP ranges, specific hardware signatures, and target Merchant Category Codes (MCC). Furthermore, agents must never have access to clear card tokens; they must push requests to an isolated Gatekeeper Enclave running deterministic boundary code.
  • For Processing Merchants: Capture and cryptographically sign an immutable setup manifest during the initial consumer onboarding session. If a chargeback claim lands, this signed manifest provides airtight evidence to networks to defeat dispute routing instantly. Concurrently, use modern fraud platforms to identify sub-second machine behavior and trigger step-up challenges when interaction profiles break human bounds.

The Human-Centric Legacy: Why SCA Rejects the Machine

To understand why AI agents break the current and future regulatory rails, we have to look at the anatomy of Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) Introduced under PSD2 and reinforced under the incoming PSR, SCA mandates that electronic transactions must be verified using at least two of three distinct factors:

Factor 1
Knowledge (Something you know)
Passwords, PINs, or memorized challenge responses. While an AI agent can store a credential, sharing static secrets lacks the dynamic, session-bound verification required by modern risk engines.
Factor 2
Possession (Something you have)
A hardware token, a registered mobile device, or a secure enclave. An AI agent running on cloud infrastructure does not "possess" a physical device in the manner defined by EBA[4] regulatory technical standards.

The third factor—Inherence (something you are), such as biometrics—is biologically impossible for a machine. Because SCA was constructed to ensure that a conscious human is explicitly approving the transfer of funds at the moment of the transaction, the entire technical loop is designed to reject automated software.

HUMAN-CENTRIC SCA (DESIGN INTENT)
  • Active human present at checkout screen
  • Biometric check (fingerprint/face scan) required
  • One-Time Passcode (OTP) sent to physical device
  • Dynamic linking to specific merchant & amount
  • Transaction-specific friction is acceptable
Autonomous Agent Execution(SCA BLOCKERS)
  • Headless background execution via cloud server
  • No biological inherence capability
  • Continuous real-time API-driven payments
  • Dynamic decisioning based on micro-parameters
  • Zero-friction requirement for scalability

The Delegated Authentication Loophole: Proximity Without Solution

Payments regulatory specialists often point to Delegated Authentication as the potential savior. Under PSD3 and the PSR proposal, delegated authentication is formally standardized. This allows an issuing bank to delegate the authentication process to a third-party wallet (like Apple Pay) or a merchant's app.

However, this framework still requires the delegating party to be a human. Under PSR Article 51 (as per draft PSR, subject to final adoption), the delegating wallet must still perform SCA on the human user at the point of action.

Some developers try to bypass this by utilizing Merchant-Initiated Transactions (MITs)[6], such as subscriptions or variable recurring payments (VRPs). While MITs allow a transaction to be initiated without the customer being present (often exempt from SCA after the initial mandate setup), they are restricted by rigid parameters:

1
Fixed Relationship Restrictions

MITs require a pre-established contract between the payer and a specific merchant. If an AI agent discovers a new merchant with a better price, it cannot execute an MIT because no prior mandate exists with that specific merchant entity.

2
Strict Velocity and Amount Caps

Variable Recurring Payments (VRPs) designed for open banking have strict limits on single transaction values, cumulative daily/monthly volumes, and transaction velocity. A procurement agent negotiating enterprise deals will quickly breach these thresholds.

3
The "Consent" Liability Trap

If an AI agent initiates an out-of-scope payment, the PSP cannot legally classify it as a merchant-initiated charge. It defaults to a cardholder-initiated transaction, making it subject to immediate "soft decline" or customer dispute.

The Regulatory Friction: BaFin, EBA, and the AI Signature Problem

This paradox creates the AI Signature Problem: when a machine makes a purchasing decision and executes a transaction, who is legally responsible?

Under PSR Article 59 (as per draft PSR, subject to final adoption), payment service providers (PSPs) are strictly liable for unauthorized transactions. If an AI agent goes rogue—perhaps due to a hallucination or an adversarial prompt injection—and spends €10,000 on the wrong asset, the payer will claim they did not authorize the specific transaction.

Regulators like Germany's BaFin[7] and Switzerland's FINMA[8] are looking at this through the lens of operational risk:

Supervisory Alert: The Consent Void

Consent vs. Delegation: Under European payment law, consent must be given "by the payer." Regulators like BaFin argue that an algorithm cannot possess legal intent (*Rechtswille*). Therefore, a machine cannot "consent" to a payment. Consent must reside in the human's setup of the delegation contract, but this setup must cryptographically bind the agent's actions to prevent liability evasion.

If the human sets up a loose delegation mandate and the agent goes off-rails, is the transaction "authorized"? If yes, the user loses consumer protection. If no, the bank or the merchant platform (routing through payment orchestration platforms) faces unsustainable chargeback and liability risks.

The Path Forward: Designing "Machine-SCA" Frameworks

If we want to build a scaling infrastructure for platforms utilizing payment orchestration platforms, we need a new standard of tokenization. We must design a cryptographic Secure Machine Authentication (SMA)[10] token.

In this model, the human performs SCA once to establish and sign a Cryptographic Delegated Mandate (CDM). The CDM is not a simple pre-authorized debit card; it is a smart contract or secure cryptographic payload that contains predefined variables:

When the AI agent initiates a transaction, it compiles the transaction payload (merchant, amount, currency) and signs it using its private key (matching the `public_key` defined in the mandate).

The merchant gateway sends this payload and the cryptographic signature to the payment orchestrator (such as ProcessOut), which routes it to the issuing bank. The issuer's HSM (Hardware Security Module) validates that:

  • The transaction details fall within the mandate's strict constraints.
  • The agent's private key signature is valid.
  • The parent mandate was established via a valid human SCA event within the allowed duration.

If all three checks pass, the issuer performs a risk-based exemption approval—utilizing Transaction Risk Analysis (TRA)[9] protocols—to bypass the interactive human 3DS challenge.

Terms & Definitions

[1] Strong Customer Authentication (SCA): A regulatory security standard under European law requiring multi-factor authentication using factors: knowledge, possession, and inherence.

[2] 3D Secure (3DS): A standardized messaging protocol used to authenticate cardholder identity during online card transactions, preventing unauthorized fraud.

[3] PSD3 & PSR: The Third Payment Services Directive (governing licensing and rules) and Payment Services Regulation (establishing uniform direct-acting rules) that modernize Europe's open banking and authentication laws.

[4] European Banking Authority (EBA): The independent EU authority that mandates technical standards for payments, banking supervision, and regulatory compliance.

[5] Variable Recurring Payments (VRPs): An open banking technology that allows third-party providers to initiate automated, recurring payments from a customer's account under agreed parameters.

[6] Merchant-Initiated Transactions (MITs): Transactions triggered by the merchant (like recurring SaaS subscriptions) without the direct involvement of the payer, following a pre-authorized setup.

[7] BaFin: The German Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (*Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht*), known for its highly rigorous oversight of payment networks.

[8] FINMA: Specifically, FINMA views autonomous agent delegation through the prism of its operational risk guidelines, expecting Swiss institutions to integrate robust algorithmic controls into their real-time infrastructures. With the expansion of the SIC5 instant payment framework under ISO 20022, the regulator focuses heavily on ensuring that these machine-driven transactions undergo continuous risk and limit validation directly within the payment stream.

[9] Transaction Risk Analysis (TRA): An exemption path under EBA technical standards enabling merchants to skip SCA redirect challenges for online transactions evaluated as highly secure and low-fraud.

[10] Secure Machine Authentication (SMA): A proposed cryptographic framework enabling non-human software agents to sign and prove payment compliance within pre-agreed human-authenticated mandates.
