The PSD3 / PSR Paradox: Why AI Agents Are About to Break Strong Customer Authentication

Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) was built on the fundamental assumption that a human is looking at a screen to authorise a transaction. What happens when an autonomous AI Agent makes a real-time purchasing decision? Here is why Europe's next payment frameworks are on a collision course with the machine economy.

By Soma Bhagat
11 min read

Imagine this: It is 2:00 AM. An autonomous AI procurement agent—orchestrated by a B2B SaaS treasury framework like the ones **Cobalt** is building—detects a sudden 15% pricing discount on a critical API cloud service. The agent makes a real-time purchasing decision to prepay for bulk credits, dynamically routing the payment through a payment orchestration platform (POP) like **IXOPAY** or **optile** to optimize transaction costs.

The transaction hits the payment gateway for €8,500. The gateway triggers a 3D Secure (3DS) challenge. It demands a Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) factor: a face scan, a fingerprint, or a one-time passcode (OTP) sent to a mobile phone.

But there is no human at the screen. The AI agent cannot feel a biometric sensor, nor can it open an SMS app. The transaction times out. The bargain is lost. The autonomous economy is frozen.

This is the **PSD3 / PSR Paradox**. The upcoming Third Payment Services Directive (PSD3) and Payment Services Regulation (PSR)—drafted by the European Commission and governed by the European Banking Authority (EBA)—aim to eradicate fraud and strengthen security. Yet, because these regulations are designed around *human-centric authentication*, they are about to make autonomous machine-to-machine payments practically illegal in Europe.

The Human-Centric Legacy: Why SCA Rejects the Machine

To understand why AI agents break the current and future regulatory rails, we have to look at the anatomy of **Strong Customer Authentication (SCA)**. Introduced under PSD2 and reinforced under the incoming PSR, SCA mandates that electronic transactions must be verified using at least two of three distinct factors:

Factor 1
Knowledge (Something you know)
Passwords, PINs, or memorized challenge responses. While an AI agent can store a credential, sharing static secrets lacks the dynamic, session-bound verification required by modern risk engines.
Factor 2
Possession (Something you have)
A hardware token, a registered mobile device, or a secure enclave. An AI agent running on cloud infrastructure does not "possess" a physical device in the manner defined by standard EBA regulatory technical standards.

The third factor—**Inherence (something you are)**, such as biometrics—is biologically impossible for a machine. Because SCA was constructed to ensure that a conscious human is explicitly approving the transfer of funds at the *moment of the transaction*, the entire technical loop is designed to eject automated software.

Human-Centric SCA (PSD2/PSD3)
  • Active human present at checkout screen
  • Biometric check (fingerprint/face scan) required
  • One-Time Passcode (OTP) sent to physical device
  • Dynamic linking to specific merchant & amount
  • Transaction-specific friction is acceptable
Autonomous Agent Execution
  • Headless background execution via cloud server
  • No biological inherence capability
  • Continuous real-time API-driven payments
  • Dynamic decisioning based on micro-parameters
  • Zero-friction requirement for scalability

The Delegated Authentication Loophole: Close, But No Cigar

Payments regulatory specialists often point to **Delegated Authentication** as the potential savior. Under PSD3 and the PSR proposal, delegated authentication is formally standardized. This allows an issuing bank to delegate the authentication process to a third-party wallet (like Apple Pay) or a merchant's app.

However, this framework still requires the delegating party to be a **human**. Under PSR Article 51, the delegating wallet must still perform SCA on the human user at the point of action.

Some developers try to bypass this by utilizing **Merchant-Initiated Transactions (MITs)**, such as subscriptions or variable recurring payments (VRPs). While MITs allow a transaction to be initiated without the customer being present (often exempt from SCA after the initial mandate setup), they are restricted by rigid parameters:

1
Fixed Relationship Restrictions

MITs require a pre-established contract between the payer and a *specific* merchant. If an AI agent discovers a new merchant with a better price, it cannot execute an MIT because no prior mandate exists with that specific merchant entity.

2
Strict Velocity and Amount Caps

Variable Recurring Payments (VRPs) designed for open banking have strict limits on single transaction values, cumulative daily/monthly volumes, and transaction velocity. A procurement agent negotiating enterprise deals will quickly breach these thresholds.

3
The "Consent" Liability Trap

If an AI agent initiates an out-of-scope payment, the PSP cannot legally classify it as a merchant-initiated charge. It defaults to a cardholder-initiated transaction, making it subject to immediate "soft decline" or customer dispute.

The Regulatory Friction: BaFin, the EBA, and the AI Signature Problem

This paradox creates the **"AI Signature Problem"**: when a machine makes a purchasing decision and executes a transaction, who is legally responsible?

Under PSR Article 59, payment service providers (PSPs) are strictly liable for unauthorized transactions. If an AI agent goes rogue—perhaps due to a hallucination or an adversarial prompt injection—and spends €10,000 on the wrong asset, the payer will claim they did not authorize the specific transaction.

Regulators like Germany's **BaFin** and Switzerland's **FINMA** are looking at this through the lens of operational risk:

Supervisory Alert: The Consent Void

Consent vs. Delegation: Under European payment law, consent must be given "by the payer." Regulators like BaFin argue that an algorithm cannot possess legal intent (*Rechtswille*). Therefore, a machine cannot "consent" to a payment. Consent must reside in the human's setup of the delegation contract, but this setup must cryptographically bind the agent's actions to prevent liability evasion.

If the human sets up a loose delegation mandate and the agent goes off-rails, is the transaction "authorized"? If yes, the user loses consumer protection. If no, the bank or the merchant platform (routing through orchestrators like **IXOPAY** or **optile**) faces unsustainable chargeback and liability risks.

Key Drivers in the European Market

The European payments landscape is shifting rapidly. Two structural trends are driving modern orchestration adoption at the time of writing:

Driver 1
Wero & EPI Integration
The European Payments Initiative (EPI) is rolling out **Wero** to replace local legacy systems like Giropay. Modern orchestrators in Europe are actively positioning themselves to offer seamless routing for this new account-to-account (A2A) standard.
Driver 2
Sovereign Data & Compliance
Under GDPR and strict local banking interpretations in Germany, independent orchestrators that offer vaulting and tokenization outside of the primary PSP's infrastructure are highly valued to avoid vendor lock-in.

The Path Forward: Designing "Machine-SCA" Frameworks

If we want to build a scaling infrastructure for companies like **Cobalt** or payments platforms like **IXOPAY** and **optile**, we need a new standard of tokenization. We must design a cryptographic **Secure Machine Authentication (SMA)** token.

In this model, the human performs SCA *once* to establish and sign a **Cryptographic Delegated Mandate (CDM)**. The CDM is not a simple pre-authorized debit card; it is a smart contract or secure cryptographic payload that contains predefined variables:

Proposed JSON Schema: Cryptographic Delegated Mandate (CDM) Token
{
  "mandate_id": "cmd_987654321_b2b_procure",
  "issuer_signature": "0x89ab...ef01", 
  "payer": {
    "account_iban": "DE89100010001234567890",
    "authorized_by_sca": "2026-06-02T14:22:00Z"
  },
  "authorized_agent": {
    "agent_id": "cobalt_treasury_bot_09",
    "public_key": "0x02bf...34a9"
  },
  "constraints": {
    "max_amount_per_tx": "10000.00",
    "currency": "EUR",
    "allowed_merchant_categories": ["4816", "7372"],
    "allowed_jurisdictions": ["EU", "CH"],
    "expiry": "2026-07-02T23:59:59Z"
  }
}

When the AI agent initiates a transaction, it compiles the transaction payload (merchant, amount, currency) and signs it using its private key (matching the `public_key` defined in the mandate).

The merchant gateway sends this payload and the cryptographic signature to the payment orchestrator (like **IXOPAY** or **optile**), which routes it to the issuing bank. The issuer's HSM (Hardware Security Module) validates that:

  • The transaction details fall within the mandate's strict constraints.
  • The agent's private key signature is valid.
  • The parent mandate was established via a valid human SCA event within the allowed duration.

If all three checks pass, the issuer performs a **risk-based exemption approval** under the transaction monitoring rules, bypassing the interactive human 3DS challenge.

The Payment Orchestrator's Opportunity

For payment orchestration platforms like **IXOPAY**, **optile**, or **BridgerPay**, solving the machine-SCA paradox is the ultimate enterprise differentiator. By building the infrastructure to ingest, validate, and pass these cryptographic mandate payloads directly to issuers, orchestrators can offer merchants **frictionless machine checkout channels**.

As corporate treasury models transition to autonomous liquidity management, the platforms that support **Secure Machine Authentication** will capture the high-velocity volume of the autonomous B2B economy, leaving legacy gateways behind.

Conclusion: Bridging the Regulation Gap

The European Banking Authority and the authors of PSD3 are currently focusing on human-centric fraud prevention. But payment networks, issuers, and orchestration layers must lobby for the regulatory recognition of **cryptographic delegation**.

The companies that succeed in the next decade won't just build the best AI agents—they will build the **regulatory bridges** that allow those agents to legally and securely spend money.
