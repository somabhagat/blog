PSD1, PSD2, PSD3: 15 Years of European Payment Regulation Explained

From a fragmented patchwork of national rules to a continent-wide open banking framework — and now toward the age of AI-initiated transactions. Here is how Europe's three Payment Services Directives connect, what problem each one was trying to solve, and what comes after PSD3.

Regulatory Deep Dive
10 min read

In 2007, answering the question "who is allowed to offer payment services in the EU?" required a country-by-country legal analysis. In some member states you needed a banking licence. In others, a dedicated payment institution authorisation. In others still, no authorisation at all. The same activity, the same service, operated under entirely different legal frameworks depending on which side of a border you sat.

That was the problem PSD1 set out to solve. Nearly two decades later, we are on the third iteration of that directive — and the challenge it is trying to address has shifted from who can offer payment services, to how do we regulate payment services initiated by machines.

Each directive tells the story of the payments era that produced it. Together, they form one of the most important regulatory narratives in modern fintech.

SCA deadline September 2019, extended to December 2020 due to implementation challenges. Open banking APIs live across EU — but highly fragmented in quality and format between banks.

PSD3 transposition and enforcement
Expected to come into effect approximately 2026, with accelerated 6-month transposition period for member states — compared to PSD2's two-year window.

PSD1: solving the fragmentation problem

Before PSD1, the answer to "what licence do I need to offer payment services in Germany vs France vs Italy?" was: ask three different lawyers.

The core problem PSD1 was designed to solve was regulatory fragmentation. In the early 2000s, EU member states each had their own rules about who could offer payment services and under what conditions. This made it nearly impossible to build a pan-European payment business. A company that wanted to operate across the EU effectively needed to navigate 25 different national legal frameworks — each with different capital requirements, different consumer protection rules, and different notions of what a "payment service" even was.

PSD1 resolved this by creating a single legal category: the Payment Institution (PI). Any company meeting the capital and risk management requirements could apply for a PI licence in any EU member state and then "passport" that licence into all other member states without separate authorisations. For the first time, it was possible to build a payment business under a single regulatory framework across the entire EU.

What PSD1 did not do was open banking. Banks remained in full control of their infrastructure. Third-party payment providers were already operating, but in a legal grey zone. PSD1 did not regulate them. That gap, and the rapid growth of digital payments between 2009 and 2015, made a revision inevitable.

PSD2: the open banking revolution

By 2013, the problem was clear: the payments landscape had changed dramatically since PSD1. Mobile payments were exploding. Fintech companies were building services that allowed customers to initiate payments or share account information with third parties — but without any regulatory framework governing how they did it. Banks had no obligation to provide access. Consumers had no protection if something went wrong.

PSD2 addressed this with three major innovations that fundamentally reshaped European payments.

🔓
Open banking mandate

PSD2 required banks to provide secure API access to licensed third-party providers. For the first time, a fintech company with a PISP or AISP licence had a legal right to access a customer's bank account — with consent — regardless of whether the bank wanted to provide that access. The monopoly on banking infrastructure was broken.

🔐
Strong Customer Authentication (SCA)

PSD2 mandated that electronic payments require at least two of three authentication factors: something you know (PIN, password), something you have (phone, token), or something you are (biometric). SCA existed before PSD2, but PSD2 made it legally mandatory across the entire EU, harmonising security standards for the first time.

🌍
Extended geographical scope

PSD1 only applied to transactions within the EEA. PSD2 extended coverage to transactions involving third countries — any payment where at least one payment service provider is located within the EU. This brought cross-border payments into the regulatory framework for the first time.

PSD2 was transformative — but its implementation exposed new problems. The most significant was API fragmentation. PSD2 mandated that banks provide APIs, but did not mandate a single standard. The result was hundreds of different API implementations, each with different formats, different documentation quality, and different reliability. Some TPPs responded by focusing only on the largest banks — inadvertently disadvantaging customers of smaller institutions.

PSD2 opened the doors to open banking. But it forgot to specify how wide the doors should open, what format the door handles should be, or whether the doors would be maintained once installed.

PSD3: evolution, not revolution

The European Commission was explicit about its ambitions for PSD3: "an evolution, not a revolution." PSD3 does not tear up the PSD2 framework — it extends, tightens, and in several places corrects it. The structural architecture of open banking remains. The licence categories remain. The SCA principle remains. What changes is the precision and scope of implementation.

Key PSD3 changes in plain terms
  • Banks must publish quarterly API performance data — uptime, latency, error rates. Poor APIs can no longer hide behind opaque compliance
  • Digital wallet providers and technical infrastructure companies now share fraud liability if they fail to apply SCA — not just the issuing bank
  • Two authentication factors from the same category are now permitted — two passwords instead of password plus biometric. Accessibility for elderly and disabled users explicitly addressed
  • Merchant-initiated transactions (subscriptions, recurring payments) exempt from per-transaction SCA — only the first transaction requires authentication
  • IBAN-name verification mandatory for all credit transfers — the UK and Netherlands have had this for years; PSD3 extends it across the EU
  • Non-bank PSPs gain the right to hold accounts at central banks — removing a key dependency that legacy banks have exploited
  • Spoofing fraud refund rights: consumers deceived by scammers impersonating bank staff are entitled to refunds

The similarities that run through all three

⚖
Consumer protection is non-negotiable

Every directive has placed consumer rights at its centre — refund rights, liability frameworks, information transparency. The principle that consumers must be protected from unauthorised payments has been constant from PSD1 to PSD3.

🏛
Directives, not regulations

All three are directives — guidelines to member states, not directly applicable law. Each country transposes into national legislation, creating room for both alignment and divergence. Switzerland operates entirely outside this framework, choosing a voluntary market-driven approach instead.

🔄
Level playing field intention

Each directive has been driven by reducing the monopoly of traditional banks over payment infrastructure. PSD1 opened the door. PSD2 forced banks to share the keys. PSD3 installs a security system that everyone must use.

What comes next: beyond PSD3

The fourth era: agentic payments and Open Finance

PSD3 is already grappling with the edges of what PSD2 was designed for. But the payments landscape is moving faster than any legislative cycle. The Santander–Mastercard AI agent pilot — Europe's first live end-to-end agentic payment — was completed in early 2026*, likely before PSD3 is even fully in force. A fourth regulatory era is already forming, even as the third is being written.

Open Finance is the immediate next frontier. Where PSD2 and PSD3 focus on payment accounts, Open Finance extends the same data-sharing principles to investment accounts, insurance policies, pensions, and mortgages. The European Commission's Open Finance Framework is in development alongside PSD3, and the two will need to work as an integrated system.

AI agent liability frameworks will be the defining regulatory challenge of the late 2020s. When an AI agent initiates a payment autonomously — within a pre-approved mandate, using cryptographic credentials, without a human in the loop — the existing PSD liability model needs fundamental rethinking. PSD4, or a supplementary AI payments regulation, will eventually need to answer these questions.

The EU AI Act interaction adds a second regulatory layer. High-risk AI systems used in payment decisions may require conformity assessments, transparency documentation, and human oversight mechanisms — obligations that sit entirely outside the PSD framework but apply to the same systems. Payments professionals will increasingly need to navigate both regimes simultaneously.

PSD1 asked who can offer payments. PSD2 asked how they can access banking data. PSD3 asks how we fix what PSD2 broke. The next question — perhaps asked by PSD4 — is who is responsible when the payment is made by a machine.
