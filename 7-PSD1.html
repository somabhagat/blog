PSD1: The Directive That Built the Foundation of European Payments

Before PSD2 could open banking, before PSD3 could fix what PSD2 broke, someone had to answer a more fundamental question: who is even allowed to move money in Europe? PSD1 answered that — and in doing so, changed the continent's financial landscape forever.

In late 2007, the European Union published a directive that most consumers never heard of, most journalists never covered, and most people in fintech today could not name without looking it up. It was called the Payment Services Directive — or PSD1 as it is now known, after its successor arrived in 2015.

It was not glamorous legislation. It did not have the fintech narrative of open banking. It did not mandate APIs. It did not require banks to share their data. What it did was more fundamental: it created a single legal framework for who could offer payment services across 30 countries, under what conditions, with what consumer protections, and with what obligations. It laid the floor on which everything that followed was built.

Without PSD1, there is no Payment Institution licence. Without the PI licence, there is no Payoneer, no Wise, no early Revolut. Without those companies, there is no pressure on banks to innovate, no competition that forces the industry forward, and no political will to write PSD2. PSD1 is not the interesting chapter of the story — but it is the first chapter, and the first chapter determines everything.

Europe before PSD1: a continent of 27 different payment laws

To understand why PSD1 mattered, you have to understand what it replaced — or more accurately, what it replaced the chaos of.

In the early 2000s, offering payment services across multiple European countries was a legal labyrinth. There was no common definition of what a "payment service" was. There was no common category of entity that could provide one. Depending on which EU member state you operated in, the same activity — receiving money from one person and transmitting it to another — might require a full banking licence, an electronic money institution authorisation, a dedicated payment institution permit, or in some countries, no licence at all.

Europe before PSD1 (pre-2007)
  • 27 different national regulatory frameworks
  • No common definition of "payment service"
  • Non-banks could not legally offer payments in most states
  • Cross-border transfers expensive and slow
  • No consumer refund rights for unauthorised transactions
  • No maximum execution time requirements
  • Banks held complete monopoly on payment infrastructure
  • No passporting — separate licence needed per country
Europe after PSD1 (post-2009)
  • Single harmonised legal framework across EU/EEA
  • Clear legal definition of payment services (8 categories)
  • Non-banks could obtain Payment Institution licence
  • Cross-border charges equalised with domestic
  • Liability framework and refund rights for consumers
  • Next-day execution required (from January 2012)
  • Market opened to competition for first time
  • Single PI licence passports across all EU/EEA states

A company that wanted to offer cross-border payments across five European countries faced five separate licensing processes, five sets of capital requirements, five different consumer protection regimes, and five different enforcement authorities — even if the product was identical in each market. The result was that only the very largest banks could realistically operate at European scale. Everyone else was locked out.

The same activity — receiving money from one person and sending it to another — might require a banking licence in Germany, an EMI permit in France, and nothing at all in some other member states. That was the world PSD1 was designed to fix.

The political moment: why 2007?

Directives do not emerge from a vacuum. PSD1 was the product of a decade of European political ambition, shaped by the Lisbon Agenda and the push to make Europe the world's most competitive knowledge economy.

The foundational political commitment was the Lisbon Agenda, agreed by European heads of state in March 2000. The agenda set a ten-year goal: make Europe the world's most competitive and dynamic knowledge-driven economy by 2010. A fragmented payment system was incompatible with that ambition. You cannot have a single digital market if you cannot make a single digital payment.

The European Payments Council's creation of SEPA in 2002 provided the industry-led counterpart to this political ambition. But self-regulation without legal backing only goes so far. Banks needed a legal framework that required all participants to operate under the same rules — and that framework was PSD1. The Commission published its proposal in December 2005. After two years of negotiation, the final text entered into force on 25 December 2007.

The two pillars of PSD1

Pillar One
Market rules — who can play
Defined which organisations could provide payment services and under what licensing conditions. Created the new Payment Institution category. Established passporting rights. Set capital requirements and risk management obligations for non-bank payment providers.
Pillar Two
Conduct rules — on what terms
Specified what information payment service providers must give customers (fees, execution times, exchange rates, references). Defined rights and obligations of both providers and users. Established liability frameworks, refund rights, and execution time requirements.

The Payment Institution: PSD1's most important creation

The most consequential thing PSD1 did was create a new legal category that had not existed before anywhere in Europe: the Payment Institution (PI).

Before PSD1, if you wanted to legally move money in Europe, you needed a banking licence. The PI licence changed this fundamentally. It created a lower-capital, lighter-touch regulatory category specifically designed for non-bank companies whose core activity was payment services. A company with a PI licence could legally initiate and execute payments, hold customer funds (with restrictions), and passport those services across every EU and EEA member state — all under a single regulatory home.

1
Apply in one member state

A company applied for PI authorisation in any EU or EEA member state where it was established. The home authority conducted the prudential review — capital adequacy, governance, risk management, business plan.

2
Meet minimum capital requirements

PI capital requirements ranged from €20,000 to €125,000 depending on service type — a fraction of a full banking licence. Ongoing own funds requirements were calculated based on payment volumes.

3
Passport across the EEA

Once authorised, the PI notified its home regulator of its intention to provide services in other member states. No separate licence, no separate capital, no separate application. The single authorisation covered 30 countries.

4
Safeguard customer funds

PIs were required to hold customer funds in a separate account at a credit institution, or cover them with an insurance policy. Customer funds were ring-fenced from the PI's own operational funds and protected in the event of insolvency.

5
Comply with conduct obligations

PI status came with ongoing obligations: transparent pricing, execution within defined timeframes, liability for unauthorised transactions, refund rights. The conduct framework applied equally to banks and PIs — the level playing field PSD1 was designed to create.

Consumer protection: the rights PSD1 created

The conduct pillar of PSD1 was as important as the market access pillar. For many European consumers, it was the first time their rights as payment service users were clearly defined in law.

📋
Transparency before the transaction

PSD1 required payment service providers to disclose specific information before a transaction was initiated: fees charged, exchange rates applied, reference numbers, and maximum execution time. For cross-border payments — where fees had previously been opaque — this was a significant protection.

⏱
Execution time limits

PSD1 set a mandatory deadline: by January 2012, all electronic payments within the EEA must execute no later than the next business day. This ended the practice of banks holding funds in transit for two to five days — earning float income on money nominally "in transit."

🛡
Liability for unauthorised transactions

Payment service providers bear liability for unauthorised transactions. If a customer's account was debited without their authorisation, the provider was required to refund immediately. The consumer's maximum liability was capped at €150 unless gross negligence or fraud could be proven.

↩
Refund rights for direct debits

For direct debit payments where amounts could vary, PSD1 introduced the right to a refund of authorised transactions within eight weeks. A consumer debited an unexpected amount could request a refund without needing to prove fraud — particularly significant for recurring payments.

The passporting effect: a continent of new entrants

The PI licence and passporting framework created a new game entirely. For the first time, a startup in Berlin, Dublin, or Amsterdam could obtain a single PI authorisation and serve customers across 30 countries without a network of country offices, local lawyers, or separate licensing processes in each market.

What PSD1 was — and what it was not

PSD1 scope — what was and was not included
  • PSD1 applied to electronic payments within the EEA — it did not cover cash, cheques, or transactions to/from outside the EEA
  • It did not mandate APIs or bank data sharing — that came with PSD2. PSD1 created the licensing framework; it did not force banks to open their infrastructure
  • It did not mandate Strong Customer Authentication — SCA was introduced by PSD2's regulatory technical standards
  • Cross-border charge equalisation was handled by EC Regulation 924/2009 — a companion regulation, not PSD1 itself
  • SEPA Credit Transfer standardisation was handled by the European Payments Council and then SEPA Regulation 260/2012
  • It was a maximum harmonisation directive — meaning member states could not impose stricter requirements, but had flexibility on certain options

The gaps PSD1 left — which PSD2 had to fill

⚠
Third-party payment providers in a legal grey zone

Companies facilitating online payments by accessing a customer's home banking application operated with no regulatory oversight. Not licensed as PIs (they did not hold funds), but not banks either. PSD1 had no category for them. PSD2 created the PISP licence.

⚠
Account information services unregulated

Companies aggregating a customer's bank accounts operated without any legal framework governing data access or customer protections. PSD2's AISP licence resolved this.

⚠
No API mandate

PSD1 created the right for non-banks to access the payments market, but gave them no mechanism to access bank account data. Banks remained gatekeepers to their own infrastructure — making the PI licence less useful than it appeared.

⚠
EEA-only scope missed growing cross-border flows

Payments to or from third countries fell entirely outside PSD1. A consumer paying a US company had no PSD1 protections. PSD2 extended coverage to transactions involving at least one EU-based payment service provider.

⚠
Inconsistent national transposition

Member states used their implementation flexibility differently, creating variations in consumer protection and supervisory enforcement that persisted into the PSD2 era and are only partially resolved even by PSD3.

PSD1's lasting legacy

What PSD1 built that still stands today

PSD1 was superseded by PSD2 in 2018, but its core architecture — the PI licence, passporting, conduct obligations — was carried forward intact. The language of European payment regulation still uses the categories and concepts PSD1 introduced.

The PI licence is still the foundation of the European fintech industry. Every non-bank payment company operating in Europe today — from Wise to Klarna — holds either a PI licence or an EMI licence. Both are direct descendants of PSD1.

The consumer protection framework is still the baseline. PSD2 raised the bar on SCA and open banking, but it preserved and extended PSD1's consumer protections. The right to a refund for unauthorised transactions, transparency obligations, execution time limits — all were PSD1 innovations.

The passporting principle shaped the European fintech map. London, Luxembourg, Dublin, and Amsterdam emerged as fintech hubs partly because of their PI licensing infrastructure — a direct result of PSD1's passporting mechanism. Post-Brexit, the scramble of UK-licensed fintechs to obtain EU PI licences demonstrated how central this framework remains.

PSD1 did not create open banking. It did not mandate APIs. It did not introduce SCA. What it did was more fundamental: it established that non-banks have the right to exist in European payments — and gave them the legal framework to do so. Everything that followed was built on that foundation.
