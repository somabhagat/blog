Breaking: provisional political agreement reached

At 1:25 AM on 27 November 2025, the European Parliament, Council, and Commission reached provisional political agreement on PSD3 and PSR in Strasbourg. Final texts are expected in H1 2026, with PSR applying directly 20 days after OJ publication and PSD3 requiring national transposition within 18 months. Full applicability: H2 2027 to Q2/Q3 2028.

After 13 years working across payment product development at Mastercard, Wirecard, and Payoneer — and completing an IHK qualification in application development alongside an MBA in Finance — I have watched two complete regulatory cycles reshape the European payments landscape. PSD1 created the framework. PSD2 opened the rails. PSD3 and PSR are doing something more ambitious: consolidating those rails, hardening the security architecture, and introducing a structural split between licensing law and conduct law with profound implications for how payment products are built.

This post is written for the practitioner. The product manager who needs to understand the technical implications for their roadmap. The architect who needs to understand what fraud data sharing means for their systems. The compliance officer who needs to understand why the directive–regulation split matters for their legal strategy.

The structural innovation: why PSD3 + PSR is not just PSD2 + 1

The most technically significant aspect of this package is not any individual rule change — it is the decision to split the framework across two instruments with fundamentally different legal effects.

PSD2 was a single directive. The result was predictable: 27 different national implementations, each with local variations, creating exactly the fragmentation the directive was supposed to eliminate. The PSD3 + PSR package addresses this directly. PSD3 remains a directive — governing licensing and supervision where national competent authority discretion is legitimate. PSR is a regulation — governing conduct obligations where uniform application across all 27 member states is the point. A regulation does not require transposition. It becomes law automatically, simultaneously, EU-wide on the day it enters into force.

PSD2 told 27 countries what to achieve. PSR tells all 27 countries exactly what to do — in the same words, on the same day. That is the difference between a directive and a regulation, and it is the most consequential structural change in this package.

The six business problems PSD3 + PSR are designed to solve

1
Authorised Push Payment (APP) fraud — the liability gap

Under PSD2, if a consumer was deceived into authorising a fraudulent payment, the loss was largely theirs. PSR closes this by making PSPs liable for impersonation fraud and introducing mandatory consumer refund rights. Online platforms that fail to remove known fraudulent content after notification also bear liability to PSPs who reimburse defrauded customers.

2
Open banking API fragmentation — the performance problem

PSD2 mandated APIs but not performance standards. PSD3/PSR mandates quarterly API performance reporting, minimum uptime standards, and standardised interfaces as the primary access mechanism. Banks can no longer hide poor infrastructure behind nominal compliance.

3
SCA rigidity — the user experience vs. security tension

PSR introduces flexible SCA — allowing two factors from the same category, risk-based exemptions, and explicit accessibility requirements. MIT transactions (subscriptions, recurring payments) require only first-transaction SCA. MOTO transactions explicitly exempt.

4
Non-bank PSP exclusion from payment systems

PSD3 gives non-bank PSPs direct access to all EU payment systems with appropriate safeguards. Crucially, it gives them the right to hold accounts at central banks — eliminating the critical dependency on commercial banking relationships that gave incumbent banks structural leverage.

5
Payee verification gap — the IBAN fraud vector

PSR mandates IBAN-name verification (Confirmation of Payee) for ALL EU credit transfers, not just instant payments. Must be offered free of charge to consumers. Closes a straightforward fraud vector: change the IBAN in an invoice, collect the payment.

6
Regulatory fragmentation — the enforcement inconsistency

PSR's direct applicability eliminates 27 different interpretations of the same obligation. Conduct rules are identical across all member states, with harmonised sanctions and no opportunity for national variation to dilute consumer protection intent.

The SCA evolution: from binary factors to risk-based intelligence

SCA is the most operationally significant change for product teams. Understanding the shift from rule-based to risk-based authentication is essential for anyone building payment flows.

PSD2's SCA model was binary: two factors from two different categories. This created friction in low-risk scenarios, accessibility barriers for users who could not use all three factor categories, and was entirely unsuitable for AI-agent-initiated transactions where no human is present. PSR introduces a fundamentally more intelligent approach — risk-based exemptions, flexible factor combinations, and explicit MIT carve-outs that align authentication friction with actual transaction risk.

Why the industry is migrating now — not waiting

🏗
Infrastructure lead times are too long

Confirmation of Payee for all credit transfers, fraud data sharing platforms, suspicious payment blocking, API performance monitoring — none of these are configuration changes. They are 12–18 month engineering projects. An institution that waits for final text publication in mid-2026 before beginning design work will not meet 2027–2028 compliance deadlines without significant operational risk.

📊
The fraud trajectory is already forcing action

APP fraud has grown disproportionately faster than card fraud since PSD2 took effect. The losses PSR is designed to address are not hypothetical — they are current, material losses happening now. Institutions that build PSR-standard fraud systems during the transition period are reducing real losses today, not just preparing for future compliance.

⚡
The competitive advantage window is finite

PSR's uniform direct effect means that on the day it applies, every PSP faces identical requirements. The differentiation comes not from compliance itself, but from compliance quality and the product innovations that better fraud data, richer open banking APIs, and more flexible SCA enable. Early movers build those capabilities during transition; late movers spend transition on emergency remediation.

Preparation priorities for 2026
  • Map your current SCA implementation against PSR's new framework — identify which flows need redesigning for flexible factors and MIT exemptions
  • Assess Confirmation of Payee readiness — if you process credit transfers without IBAN-name verification, this is your highest-priority build
  • Review your EMI licence structure — PSD3 integrates EMIs into the PI framework. Understand the migration path and timeline
  • Engage with EBA consultation on PSR technical standards — implementation details that matter most are defined here, not in primary legislation
  • Evaluate direct payment system access — for non-bank PSPs, eliminating commercial bank intermediary is both compliance imperative and cost/risk reduction
  • Begin fraud data sharing architecture — the PSR framework requires new data infrastructure, DPIA processes, and legal agreements
  • Build or review your permission dashboard — this is a customer-facing product requirement, not a compliance tick-box

The view from 13 years of payments product experience

What PSD3 + PSR means for how payment products get built

I have been building payment products since before PSD2 was enforcement-ready. The shift from PSD2 to PSD3 + PSR feels qualitatively different from the shift from PSD1 to PSD2. PSD2 changed the market structure — who could access banking infrastructure. PSR changes the operating model — how every participant must behave, uniformly, with no national variation to exploit or hide behind.

The most significant change for product teams is not the headline fraud rules or SCA flexibility. It is the fraud data sharing framework. For the first time, PSPs will be required to share fraud intelligence through a regulated platform. That shared intelligence — combined with ISO 20022's richer transaction data and AI-driven fraud orchestration — means the fraud detection arms race is about to get significantly more sophisticated.

The PSR's permission dashboard requirement is similarly underestimated. The first PSP that builds a genuinely excellent, transparent, easy-to-use permission dashboard will have a competitive advantage that compounds as open banking adoption grows. Regulators are mandating its existence; the market will reward the best implementation of it.

And the timeline is not generous. With EBA technical standards expected throughout 2026–2027 and full applicability from H2 2027, payment product teams have roughly 18 months from today to reach a state of readiness. That is not a lot of runway for the structural engineering this framework requires.

PSD3 + PSR is not evolution for evolution's sake. It is a regulatory response to a payments market that grew 30% in four years, saw APP fraud become the dominant fraud typology, and produced open banking APIs that technically comply while practically failing. The direction is clear. The only question is whether you are building toward it — or waiting to be pulled.
